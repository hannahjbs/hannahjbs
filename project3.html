Remote Sensing of Harmful Algal Blooms

Background

Remote sensing of harmful algal blooms (HABs) could be a cost-effective alternative to water quality testing. Thanks to NASA’s Iowa Space Grant, I am evaluating the feasibility of trail cameras and satellite imagery in early detection of HABs.

Data sources include trail cameras at Big Spirit Lake as well as satellite imagery (Landsat 8, Planet, Sentinel 1 and 2). Remote sensing methods are validated against water quality testing data.

Methods

Using python and an open-source image processing package, opencv2, I wrote software to determine the “greenness” of the water in trail camera images. For a given region of interest in the image, greenness is measured on a pixel by basis, then averaged over the entire region.

However, trail camera image processing is confounded by water reflectivity, angle of cameras, angle of sun, weather conditions and choppiness of the water, among other factors. Below, the time series of "greenness" at Crandall's beach shows noisy data (black line is known algal bloom). Methods would need to be refined to gain any insights from trail camera images.

Using geopandas and rioxarray, I am processing satellite imagery of the lake. "Greenness" is quantified using maximum chlorophyll index (MCI), which was developed for measuring seasonal vegetation changes in forests. Each satellite has different instrumentation and record different spectral bands, so MCI formulas vary.

Preliminary Results

Below shows MCI calculations during 2022 using Landsat-8 images of the lake. Darker colors indicate larger MCI (ie, "greener"). More analysis is needed, however, preliminary results show an increase in MCI around the time of the known algal bloom (8/17).
